Application of Knowledge in Real-World Contexts: A Comparative Analysis of MLC and MYP Students in PISA Tests

A quantitative study examining how students from the Maverick Learning Centre (MLC) and the Middle Years Programme (MYP) — both part of Fountainhead School — compare in their ability to apply knowledge and skills to real-life contexts, as measured through PISA-based assessments across Mathematics, Science, and English.

Background & Context

Introduction

This study forms part of a larger evaluation of Maverick Learning Centre (MLC), a programme within Fountainhead School that operates predominantly on a project-based learning (PBL) model. To assess its effectiveness, an important parameter was students' ability to apply their learning in real-world scenarios — an area in which PBL is theoretically expected to excel.

Accordingly, an assessment was conducted using a diagnostic test designed by PISA (Programme for International Student Assessment), which evaluates students' ability to apply knowledge and skills to real-life situations rather than relying on memorisation or routine procedures. The tests assess competencies such as problem-solving, reasoning, interpretation, and the application of concepts across Mathematics, Science, and English.

The test was conducted with two groups for comparison: students in the MYP (Middle Years Programme) and students in MLC — both programmes within Fountainhead School. Since MYP follows the regular IB curriculum unlike MLC, it was expected that MLC students may perform comparatively better in application-oriented assessments due to their project-based learning environment. The purpose of this study was to examine whether this expectation is reflected in student performance.

Broad Research Question

How do students from the Maverick Learning Centre (MLC) and the Middle Years Programme (MYP) — both within Fountainhead School — compare in their ability to apply knowledge and skills in real-life contexts as measured through PISA-based assessments?

Research Design

Methodology

The study employed a quantitative approach to examine the performance of students from the MLC and MYP programmes of Fountainhead School based on the PISA assessments. Each of the three subjects assessed three broad skills (total 9), such as computational skills, interpretation, reflection, explaining phenomena and so on. Questions were mapped to the MYP syllabus; questions outside this alignment were excluded.

The original PISA papers did not assign direct marks to questions. Instead, official answer keys provided responses in the form of credit codes indicating levels of correctness. These were systematically converted into numerical marks by the researchers. Students were informed that it was only a diagnostic test and were not given any prior syllabus or preparation time.

Sample & Eligibility

Participants were aged 15 years 3 months to 16 years 2 months (Grades 9–11). Sample sizes varied slightly across subjects due to student availability and test completion.

Scoring Approach

Original PISA credit codes were converted into numerical scores. A detailed mapping sheet is available for reference, showing which questions align to each skill and the MYP syllabus.

Statistical Tests

  • Mann–Whitney U test — where normality was violated
  • Welch's t-test — where normality was met (robust to unequal group sizes)

Effect Size Reporting

  • Rank biserial correlation (r) — for Mann–Whitney tests
  • Cohen's d — for Welch's t-tests
Methodological Note

The comparatively small MLC sample size limits the statistical power of the analysis and reduces the ability to detect smaller differences between groups. Non-significant p-values should be interpreted in this context.

Notable Observation — Chemistry Gap

MLC students scored notably lower in Chemistry (34.6% vs 48%). This could be due to MLC not having a dedicated chemistry teacher in the past, and student projects mostly belonging to Physics and Biology domains. This points towards the need for MLC to balance projects domain-wise to allow balanced exposure to students.

1.3 Comparison Within Scientific Skills

Students scored higher in identifying scientific issues and using scientific evidence — tasks involving recognising information, reading graphs, selecting relevant evidence, and drawing direct conclusions. A lower performance was observed in explaining phenomena scientifically, which requires deeper conceptual understanding, connecting scientific ideas, causal reasoning, and applying knowledge to unfamiliar situations.

This pattern suggests that higher-order scientific reasoning and application skills are more challenging for students compared to structured and evidence-based tasks — a finding consistent across both MLC and MYP.

2.1 Comparison Within Mathematical Skills

Students of both groups performed higher in Content and Mathematical Process while scoring lowest in Reflections (Mathematical Thinking) — the ability to analyse information and make connections between mathematical models and real-world situations. This shared weakness in higher-order mathematical reasoning is a key area for targeted improvement across both institutions.

Discussion

Conclusion

Overall, the findings indicate that students from MLC and MYP demonstrated broadly similar performance in Science and English, while in Mathematics, MYP students showed comparatively stronger performance. In MLC, mathematics is taught as a core subject, rather than through a PBL approach covering the same curriculum as MYP. And therefore, one might expect a similar or even higher performance — especially since MLC students have more scope of application through projects. The earlier MLC case study research found that application of maths in projects did not appear explicitly in planning documents. So, application of mathematics in real life situations still seems at a limited scale through PBL. There's a need for further investigation for identifying the reasons and the role of teaching methods and other factors on performance.

A comparison within the three skills of all three subjects revealed that students of both groups generally performed better in structured, recognition-based, and procedural tasks than in higher-order thinking questions that required reflection, reasoning, and application — suggesting that such skills remain comparatively more challenging across both settings.

Central Implication

Due to the PBL environment, one might expect MLC students to demonstrate stronger performance in real-life application assessments. However, the findings did not show a clear or consistent advantage for MLC students. Their performance remained similar to MYP, with actual scores showing slightly lower performance in the higher-order skills in science and mathematics. This demands a critical review of the current PBL practices at MLC.

Another aspect is the role of subject content knowledge in the development of higher-order skills. Research consistently states that "the focus on thinking skills does not imply that the essential importance of knowledge is abandoned — in fact, knowledge is needed and related to thinking processes" (Loyens et al., 2023; Lombardi, 2023). Unlike MYP, MLC does not comply with any fixed subject syllabus, as that would not align with its goal of being driven by passion. Balancing domain knowledge with student agency and passion therefore remains a central challenge of program planning.

Next Steps

Recommendations

The findings highlight the need to focus more on developing higher-order thinking, explanation, reflection, and real-world application skills across subjects. Teachers should present students with explicit opportunities to work on a variety of authentic real-life situations and problems that demand the development of such skills.

  • 1
    Shift PBL from "Dessert Projects" to Central Inquiry
    The case study on MLC revealed that projects are mostly conducted at the end of a teaching phase, functioning as applied extensions rather than as the primary medium of inquiry. Repositioning projects as the central vehicle for learning — not an add-on — would better fulfil PBL's potential for developing higher-order skills. This applies not only to MLC but also to MYP personal projects.
  • 2
    Balance Projects Domain-Wise for Equitable Exposure
    MLC's notably lower Chemistry scores indicate uneven domain exposure. A more deliberate framework for project selection should ensure balanced coverage across Physics, Chemistry, and Biology to avoid skill gaps arising from curriculum blind spots.
  • 3
    Explore Complementary Pedagogical Strategies
    Apart from PBL, other teaching strategies that allow students to learn in the context of real-life situations can be explored — ensuring students encounter diverse problem types that build the full range of competencies assessed by PISA.
  • 4
    Targeted Support for Low-Performing Students
    The study data enables identification of specific students performing substantially below the average in different skills across subjects. Programmes such as the "Student Champions" initiative or other mentorship support within Fountainhead School can be designed towards the specific diagnosed needs of such students, rather than providing generic support.
